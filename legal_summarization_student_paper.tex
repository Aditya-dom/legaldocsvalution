\begin{abstract}
Legal judgments are usually long, dense, and difficult to read quickly. Because of this, legal document summarization is an important task in natural language processing. However, a summary should not only be short; it should also preserve the real meaning of the case. This paper is a short student understanding of the framework proposed by Mullick et al. for evaluating legal summaries. The main idea is that common metrics such as ROUGE focus on word overlap, while legal summaries should also capture the intent of the case. The framework therefore checks whether a summary keeps the important intent-bearing phrases linked to categories such as murder, robbery, land dispute, and corruption. What makes this work meaningful is that the proposed intent-based evaluation was reported to agree better with human judgment than standard overlap-based scoring. In our view, this makes the framework practical as well as academically interesting.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Legal documents are not like short news articles or social media posts. They often contain facts, arguments, procedural history, legal reasoning, and the final decision in the same document. A person reading a court judgment may need to know very quickly what kind of case it is and what actually happened. This is why summarization becomes useful in the legal domain.

This short paper presents our understanding of the work in \cite{mullick2022}. The problem discussed in that work is not only how to generate summaries, but also how to judge whether a summary is truly useful. A summary can have many words in common with a reference summary and still fail to mention the most important action, accusation, or outcome of the case. In law, that missing detail matters a lot. Because of that, legal summarization needs evaluation that goes beyond surface similarity.

\section{Understanding the Framework}
The framework starts from a simple but strong idea: a good legal summary should preserve case intent. Here, intent does not mean emotion or opinion. It means the phrases that show what the case is really about, such as the core offence, dispute, claim, or outcome. If a summary misses those phrases, then it may sound acceptable but still be weak from a legal point of view.

The dataset described in the repository contains 93 Indian legal documents with manually annotated intent phrases. The four main case categories are Murder, Robbery, Land Dispute, and Corruption. The repository also includes Australian legal data, where intent phrases are extracted automatically to study whether the method can generalize across jurisdictions. This design is useful because it does not limit the idea to only one legal system.

The project compares several summarization approaches available in the repository. These include a BERT-based extractive summarizer, a Graphical Model approach, LetSum, CaseSummarizer, and Legal-LED. Some of these methods extract sentences directly from the source document, while others are more generative. Even though the models are different, the important point is that all of them can be tested under the same evaluation view.

To capture intent, the framework uses a JointBERT-style setup for two related tasks: identifying the overall case category and locating important intent phrases inside the text. In simple words, the model tries to answer two questions together: what is this case about, and which words in the summary show that? This is helpful because legal summaries are not judged only by fluency. They are judged by whether they keep the legally meaningful content.

\section{Discussion}
What we find most valuable in this framework is its practical thinking. Traditional metrics like ROUGE are still useful because they are fast and widely accepted \cite{lin2004}. But they mostly reward lexical overlap. In legal writing, two summaries may use different wording while still carrying the same meaning, or they may share many words while missing the central issue. That is why intent-based evaluation feels more aligned with real use.

The repository guide also explains that the proposed metric showed better agreement with human evaluation than common overlap-based metrics. This matters because the final reader of a legal summary is a human, not an automatic script. If human evaluators think a summary misses the real case focus, then a high overlap score alone should not be trusted too much. From a student perspective, this is the strongest lesson of the paper: evaluation must match the purpose of the task.

At the same time, the framework has limits. The manually annotated legal dataset is small, which is understandable because annotation in law takes time and domain knowledge. The categories are also specific, so wider deployment would need more data and more testing. Even with these limitations, the framework feels promising because it brings legal meaning back into the evaluation process instead of reducing everything to shared words.

\section{Conclusion}
In conclusion, this framework offers a thoughtful way to look at legal document summarization. Instead of asking only whether a summary resembles a reference, it asks whether the summary preserves the intent of the case. That shift makes the evaluation more meaningful for legal documents, where the key issue and outcome are more important than simple word matching. As students, we see this work as a good example of how NLP systems become stronger when evaluation is tied to real domain needs. For legal AI, that is an important direction.
